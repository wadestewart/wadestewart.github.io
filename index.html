ABOUT

I have a passion for design and development.

I am a creative thinker that brings enthusiasm to problem-solving. After finishing my degree, I supervised the receiving side of a distribution center where I learned to manage a team and work with an inventory database, a process I really enjoyed. This is when I discovered code and started attending workshops and lectures in my free time. I moved to a role as the Operations Manager of a nonprofit that offers computer skills training to low-income adults. Here I ran a computer refurbishment program, administered a WordPress website and began learning UX/UI and design principles. I enjoyed learning about computer hardware, building refurbished computers and working with our teachers coming from the tech space.

I made an intentional shift towards web-design by taking a position as a project manager for a real estate development firm. I began coordinating a creative team that primarily produces WordPress websites, along with marketing material, print media and blogs. Here I was able to nurture my interest in code-based design by regular exposure to developers. I joined the full stack program at General Assembly and loved every minute of the challenges it provided. I am now looking to continue learning new skills and contribute on a collaborative team!

What is your favorite analog product? A pencil

Wireframing marks the beginning of the web development process. I have used pencil drawing as a first step of organizing past creative endeavors and I now apply the same exploration to web development. More pencil drawing in the planning and exploration process means fewer bugs in the final product.

What’s the best advice you’ve heard recently? Engage in your craft daily

During a development talk at Chief, an advertising agency in Washington, DC, the speaker honored Graham Greene's methodical writing regimen. The idea of a methodical, daily commitment and method to honing your chosen craft resonated with me. The concept applies to many areas in life, not just skill development.

What do you like to do when not designing or coding? Golf

Golf offers a unique personal challenge, to both mind and body. Golf is not intuitive, it takes a lot of practice and focus. The real reward in golf may be that it’s a life-long endeavor, the same could be said for coding.
